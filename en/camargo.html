THE CAMARGO DANCE OR CAMARGO THE DANCER?

...Nicolas Lancret: 'The Camargo Dancer'. That's how I first read the painting's title as a child. I was sure the image showed a dancer performing a 'Camargo'—a possibly ancient, perhaps once-popular, and likely Spanish-origin dance (judging by the colourful name).

Time passed, and the errors of my childhood misinformation were corrected. However, both fashion and Spain still found their place in this story.

We see before us a refined, poetic, and intimate portrait of **Marie-Anne de Camargo**. Her father, of Spanish descent, quickly realized his daughter was a highly talented dancer, and the art of ballet became Marie-Anne's life and her path to success.

THE REVOLUTIONARY 20 CENTIMETRES

It was talent, mastery, and inspiration—not title, status, or even beauty—that made her one of Paris’s most famous and influential women. That, and courage. And, perhaps, a dash of adventurism.

The famous Voltaire, one of Camargo's admirers, gave her what seems a strange compliment today. He said: **"She was the first to rival men in dance!"**

Voltaire forgot to add that in this competition, Camargo was at a distinct disadvantage. Men did not have to gather and lift heavy, ornate skirts during their pirouettes and steps.

The skirt itself became Camargo's fiercest enemy. And so, she shortened it. **By a revolutionary 20! centimetres.**

  • **The Scandal:** Naturally, this caused a colossal scandal. The public was split between those who accused Camargo of immorality: "She publicly displayed her legs!" and those who admired, enthused, and applauded.
  • **The Trendsetter:** Her fame soared! 'À la Camargo' shoes became fashionable among court ladies.
  • **The Victim of Fashion:** Her shoemaker became the most sought-after in Paris (the very definition of a 'victim of fashion', said with a smile).

THE CLIMAX: THE POWER OF ART AND LIGHTNESS

Night, the moon, a young man in love, a guitar, a beautiful woman, and a song-confession—that is the image that comes to mind when we talk about the musical genre of the Serenade. But! It was not always necessarily so.

In the absence of musical talent, a wealthy and passionate suitor could simply hire a singer or even an entire ensemble. The city streets would then ring with a romantic open-air concert in honour of love and beauty. **Modern gentlemen certainly have something to learn from this!**
